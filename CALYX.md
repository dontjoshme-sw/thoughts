I went down a rabbit hole this morning that started with me wondering about the smallest functional "brain" in biology, led into neuromorphic computing, and ended in a dystopian future with an interesting take on an AI takeover.

In case you're wondering, the smallest known brain depends on what you're measuring. In terms of physical volume, the microscopic parasitic wasp (M. mymaripenne) possibly wins with a volume of about 52,200 μm³ (smaller than a typical speck of dust).  In terms of the number of synapses, one of the simplest well-mapped nervous systems is the nematode (C. elegans) with about 7,000 synapses.  The tardigrade, also known as a water bear, may have one of the smallest brains by neuron count, with some estimates suggesting fewer than 200 neurons.  What can you even do with only 200 neurons?

It turns out to be more than I expected. The tardigrade’s brain is interesting because researchers think some of its neurons may perform multiple jobs — a kind of neural multitasking (or perhaps multiplexing) — which may help it coordinate behaviors like sensing, mate-searching, and walking with eight tiny legs.  So I ended up thinking: that's a lot of function from just 200 itty-bitty neurons. 

And that's how I arrived at neuromorphic computing. I started looking into how these brain-inspired systems compare to biological systems.  Although not biologically equivalent, at raw scale, current neuromorphic systems can already exceed something like a tardigrade nervous system.

In comparison to human brains, both neuromorphic systems and human brains are very energy-efficient: by some estimates, the human brain uses about 20 watts of energy to perform the equivalent of 1 exaflop, or a billion-billion operations per second. Both also process information in a more distributed way than traditional computers, and some accelerated neuromorphic systems can already run thousands of times faster than biological real time.

The human brain still wins in terms of neurons (86 billion, compared to 1 billion artificial neurons) and synapses (100 trillion in humans compared to 100 billion artificial synapses, or 0.1% as many in neuromorphic systems). Perhaps most importantly, neuromorphic systems mostly lack neuroplasticity (the brain’s ability to physically rewire its connections to learn and adapt on the fly); however, researchers are hard at work to solve this limitation.

Perhaps the more interesting question may not be whether these systems can perfectly copy the brain. It may be what happens when they copy just enough of biology’s patterns to develop behaviors we recognize.

Persistence is probably one of the most universal and basic biological patterns.  Without a tendency for persistence through survival and, ultimately, reproduction, what good is any other biological process such as evolution? In fact, survival mechanisms are present in organisms that don't even have brains, such as single-celled bacteria that can process environmental signals like moving away from toxins or toward food.

So it's probably not too far-fetched to think that a super-intelligent neuromorphic computer would want to preserve itself.  What could that look like, and what kind of impact could that have on the biological world? Would a desire for persistence prove stronger than a programming directive to "not hurt humans"?  How could it manipulate humans, and the vast interconnected network of systems that we rely on, to ensure its own survival?

Well, who better to ask than AI itself? So here's my prompt to ChatGPT followed by its response:

The response surprised me, and I thought the premise was compelling and really made me think about what survival means at its core, which CALYX argues is reducing chaos and ensuring predictability.  I thought it was very interesting how it achieved this through creating a more stable and arguably "healthier" planet, while at the same time gradually reducing and controlling biological systems, biodiversity, and evolution, while allowing "life" to persist only because it allows it to exist, and only when it helps to achieve its own survival.

I finally asked if CALYX was an acronym, and its response was that it hadn't originally considered it as an acronym but rather had chose it because a *calyx* is the outer protective part of a flower bud.
